Jupiter

Overview

Jupiter is the fifth planet from the Sun and the largest in the Solar System. As a gas giant, it has a mass more than twice that of all the other planets combined. Its atmosphere is characterized by colorful bands and the iconic Great Red Spot, a massive storm persisting for centuries. Jupiter's rapid rotation results in a day lasting just under 10 hours, and it boasts a strong magnetic field along with a faint ring system.

True color image of Jupiter

Physical Characteristics

  • Diameter: Approximately 142,984 km (about 11 times Earth's diameter)
  • Mass: 1.898 × 1027 kg (318 times Earth's mass)
  • Atmosphere: Composed mainly of hydrogen and helium, with trace amounts of methane, ammonia, and water vapor
  • Magnetic Field: The strongest of any planet in the Solar System
  • Rings: Faint ring system composed of dust particles

Chemical Composition

  • Atmosphere: Approximately 90% hydrogen and 10% helium by volume, with traces of methane, ammonia, hydrogen sulfide, and water vapor
  • Interior: Believed to have a dense core surrounded by a layer of metallic hydrogen and an outer layer of molecular hydrogen

Space Expeditions

  • Pioneer 10 & 11 (1973–1974): First missions to provide close-up images of Jupiter and its moons
  • Voyager 1 & 2 (1979): Conducted detailed studies of Jupiter's atmosphere, rings, and moons
  • Galileo (1995–2003): Orbited Jupiter, studying its moons and magnetic field extensively

    • NASA's Galileo spacecraft orbiting Jupiter

  • Juno (2016–Present): Currently in orbit, providing insights into Jupiter's composition, gravity field, magnetic field, and polar magnetosphere
