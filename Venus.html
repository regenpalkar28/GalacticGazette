Venus

Overview

Venus is the second planet from the Sun and is often called Earth's twin due to its similar size and composition. Despite these similarities, Venus is a hostile world with scorching surface temperatures, thick clouds of sulfuric acid, and crushing atmospheric pressure.

Venus rotates very slowly and in the opposite direction of most planets. A day on Venus (one full rotation) lasts longer than its year. Its thick atmosphere traps heat through a runaway greenhouse effect, making it the hottest planet in our Solar System. Venus does not have any moons.

True color image of Venus

Physical Characteristics

  • Surface Temperature: Around 465°C (869°F) both day and night
  • Surface Features: Volcanoes, large plains, highland regions, impact craters
  • Atmosphere: Extremely dense; 96% carbon dioxide with clouds of sulfuric acid
  • Pressure: 92 times that of Earth at sea level

Chemical Composition

  • Atmosphere: Mostly carbon dioxide (CO₂) and nitrogen (N₂), with trace sulfur compounds
  • Surface Composition: Basaltic rock, with evidence of past volcanic activity
  • Interior: Iron core, rocky mantle and crust similar to Earth's

Space Expeditions

  • Venera Missions (1961–1984): Soviet missions that successfully landed on Venus and transmitted data from the surface.
  • Magellan (1989–1994): NASA orbiter that mapped 98% of Venus’s surface using radar.
  • Akatsuki (Launched 2010): A Japanese mission currently orbiting Venus, studying its weather and atmosphere.
  • Upcoming Missions: NASA’s VERITAS and ESA’s EnVision, both planned to launch in the 2030s for in-depth surface and atmosphere studies.
